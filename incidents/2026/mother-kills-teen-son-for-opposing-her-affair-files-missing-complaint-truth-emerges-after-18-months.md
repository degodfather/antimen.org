# Andhra Pradesh Mother Allegedly Killed Son and Filed Missing Complaint to Conceal Crime

![](/img/incidents/mother-kills-teen-son-for-opposing-her-affair-files-missing-complaint-truth-emerges-after-18-months.png)

## Incident

**02 June 2026, Andhra Pradesh** A shocking case from Andhra Pradesh's Kurnool district came to light after investigators uncovered the alleged murder of a teenage boy who had been missing for nearly 18 months.

According to police, the boy's mother allegedly killed him with the assistance of her lover after he opposed their relationship. Investigators allege that the body was secretly buried and that the mother subsequently filed a missing person's complaint in an attempt to divert suspicion.

The case remained unresolved for over a year before a Special Investigation Team (SIT) reopened the investigation and uncovered evidence leading to the arrests of the accused.

The revelation has sparked outrage and renewed concerns about child safety and accountability in custody arrangements.

## A Broader Question

This tragedy does not imply that mothers are generally unsafe parents. The overwhelming majority of mothers care for and protect their children.

However, cases such as this raise an important question: **Should custody and guardianship decisions be based on gender-based assumptions, or on an objective assessment of parental fitness?**

In many family disputes, mothers are treated as the default custodial parent, while fathers often face significant hurdles in obtaining custody or maintaining meaningful involvement in their children's lives.

## Conclusion

The death of this teenager is first and foremost a tragedy that cost a child his life.

It also serves as a reminder that children can be harmed by either parent. Family courts and child welfare systems should evaluate mothers and fathers by the same standards, focusing on the child's safety, wellbeing, and best interests rather than assumptions based on gender.

Protecting children requires judging parents as individuals—not presuming that either parent is automatically the safer or more suitable caregiver.
